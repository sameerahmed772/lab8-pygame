For this lab, I wanted to get hands-on with Pygame by building an app where 10 squares bounce around an 800x600 window. To really understand how the game loop works, I decided to go a bit beyond the basic requirements. I added gravity, friction, and collision detection so the squares bounce off the walls and each other. I also turned it into a mini-game where you can press the spacebar to spawn new squares or click on them to score points, complete with a UI that tracks the timer, score, and FPS.

The biggest headache wasn't even the code, it was just getting Pygame to install on my Mac. It kept throwing a wheel-building error. After some digging, I found out this is a common issue with newer Macs, and switching to the Community Edition (pygame-ce) fixed it instantly without me having to change any of my code.

Coding-wise, my main hurdle was fixing this weird jittery movement. I realized that calculating physics with whole pixels causes rounding errors. Storing the exact positions as decimals and only converting them to whole numbers right when they get drawn to the screen made everything perfectly smooth. Honestly, figuring out that math trick and building the core game loop from scratch was a super fun way to learn the basics of game dev.
