For this lab, I wanted to get hands-on with Pygame by building an app where squares bounce around an 800x600 window. To really understand how the game loop works, I decided to go beyond the basic requirements. I added gravity, friction, and collision detection so the squares bounce off the walls and each other. I also turned it into a mini-game where you can press the spacebar to spawn new squares or click on them to score points, with a UI that tracks the timer, score, and FPS.

The biggest headache was just getting Pygame to install on my Mac. It kept throwing a wheel-building error. After some digging, I found out this is a common issue with newer Macs, and switching to the Community Edition (pygame-ce) fixed it instantly.

Coding-wise, my main hurdle was fixing weird jittery movement. I realized that calculating physics with whole pixels causes rounding errors. Storing the exact positions as decimals and only converting them to whole numbers right when they get drawn to the screen made everything perfectly smooth. Honestly, figuring out that math trick was a super fun way to learn the basics of game dev.

In the final phase, I added a "Chase and Flee" system. Small squares run away from big ones, but now the big squares are "predators" and actively chase the small ones! I used vector math to calculate the distance and direction. To make the game more dynamic, I also added a Lifespan feature. Every square now has a "timer" (between 30 to 180 seconds). When the time is up, the square disappears and a new one is born. This keeps the game moving and the population stable.
